TEAM 2
Bhavya, Filly, Armaan

Diverge: Design app for artists and designers to break out of algorithmic echo chambers by turning passive inspiration into an active creative process.

When artists and designers look for inspiration in their creative learning process they often turn to platforms such as Instagram or Pinterest. Because these platforms rely on engagement driven recommendation systems, they mostly show what's popular rather than what is personally meaningful or creatively useful for the artist or designer. This causes users to fall into the repetitive culture of trends and trend chasing, rather than developing their own creative process. It becomes easier to keep scrolling for references rather than to actually create. Over time the same "aesthetic categories" keep getting reinforced across different apps only making it harder to experiment and keep your work feeling original. The result is a learning environment that rewards copying what already works instead of exploring what could be original.

Level UP: A gamified coding practice platform for STEM students with instant feedback, visible progress, and rewards.

Many university students find it hard to stay focused on learning because schoolwork often feels slow, stressful, and unclear, especially compared to digital entertainment. When effort does not lead to quick and visible results, students are more likely to avoid academic tasks and choose activities that feel easier and more rewarding. Most existing solutions focus on controlling behavior instead of understanding how students emotionally experience learning. This shows that procrastination is not just about poor time management, but about how learning is structured and felt in the moment.

Week Book: A deadline journal that breathes with your stress

Students struggle with procrastination and poor time management because existing tools like Canvas only remind them of deadlines without supporting proactive learning planning, leading to last-minute stress, rushed assignments, and avoidance behaviors that undermine genuine learning. BrainSprout: Short-form video viewing learning platform that aims to turn expert knowledge into engaging bite-sized learning experiences

As short-form video platforms are evolving dramatically and have dominated media consumption, people are losing the ability to think deeply, pay attention, and erode their deep learning capabilities. While instructors attempt to increase engagement and attention through long lectures and educational videos, these videos mostly just reinforce the surface understanding level. Students/learners only pay short-term attention to those videos without actually engaging in the lectures and the content. Additionally, as short-form educational videos opt for convenience rather than reflection or critical thinking, they lack interaction for learners to focus on and engage in. Because of this, students/learners may have brief moments of engagement but find it difficult to maintain focus and gain a deeper comprehension of academic content, leading to short attention span and short-term memory.

BridgeUW: An app where you set goals, build skills, and connect with mentors to prepare for your future career

In conclusion, undergraduate students in biomedical and health informatics at the University of Washington lack a cohesive learning experience that meaningfully combines computer science, information systems, and biological sciences. While students gain specific knowledge through coursework, they are usually left to independently combine these concepts across fields without structured guidance, like other majors. This leads to gaps between understanding concepts in class and real-world application, leaving students feeling unprepared for research, internships, and careers in this field. While there are a few BIME (Biomedical informatics and education) courses that allow students to declare their major as Informatics, with a specialization in biomedical and health informatics, there aren’t enough resources and assistance to students who want to pursue this career path at the University of Washington. Therefore, there is a clear need for a design that supports integrative thinking, builds confidence and clarity, and helps students apply knowledge across all fields and in the workforce.

Aligned: A Learning Platform that Prioritizes Understanding Over Completion

Online learning platforms play a central role in K–12 education, yet they often rely on completion-based metrics and one size fits all feedback to represent student learning. These indicators can mask shallow understanding, allowing students to appear successful while struggling with core concepts. Because teachers depend on platform data to guide instruction, this misrepresentation makes it difficult to identify when students need support. Addressing this problem requires rethinking how learning progress and understanding are communicated within online learning environments so that learning, not just task completion, is done.

StudyFlow: A study management platform that helps students plan, focus, and track their learning by integrating study planning, focus sessions, and academic assistance into one unified workflow.

AI tutoring tools in K-12 education currently define learning too narrowly by focusing on correctness and speed, while ignoring students’ emotional experiences during learning. This creates a gap where frustration, anxiety, or disengagement go unrecognized, leading to negative learning cycles rather than persistence and growth. Younger students, who are still developing emotional regulation skills, are especially impacted by systems that provide repetitive corrective feedback without emotional support. The core problem is not a lack of personalized content, but a lack of emotionally responsive design that supports motivation, pacing, and reflection as part of the learning process. Addressing this problem requires rethinking how AI tutors interpret and respond to student struggle as part of learning, not just performance.

TEAM 23
Yang Li, Ziyi Wang, Frank Shi

Senior ScamGuard Simulator: Help older adults build confidence and develop practical habits for responding to scams.

In today's digital age, older adults increasingly need to learn how to use digital technologies safely. However, many older adults lack effective opportunities to develop the skills to identify AI-driven scams. With AI-generated voice cloning and deepfake scams becoming increasingly sophisticated, existing safeguards and information resources are insufficient to effectively help older adults learn how to recognize and respond to these threats. This creates a learning gap, where older adults are expected to navigate a high-risk digital environment independently but lack sufficient practice, feedback, and confidence-building experiences. The core of the problem is not just the existence of AI scams, but the lack of skills among older adults to identify these scams—skills we aim to help them develop.
